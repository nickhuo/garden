# Do Things that Don't Scale — Paul Graham (July 2013)

## Central thesis
Startups don't take off by themselves; **the founders make them take off**. Early on, founders
must do labor-intensive, unscalable things — recruit users one by one, deliver obsessive customer
service, focus on a deliberately narrow market — to generate the momentum that later, scalable
growth depends on. These inefficient practices are not detours; they teach the founders what to
build and often become permanent company DNA.

## Sections / ideas

### Recruit (manual user acquisition)
You cannot wait for users to come to you — the "better mousetrap" assumption (great product →
beaten path to your door) is false. Founders must go out and get users by hand. Even Stripe, which
solved an urgent problem, recruited aggressively. Sam Altman's note: you can't delegate sales away
in the beginning; hiring someone doesn't exempt founders from doing it themselves.

### The Collison installation
Stripe's founders (the Collison brothers) didn't just ask people to try it later. When someone
agreed, they'd say **"Right then, give me your laptop"** and set it up on the spot. Removing the
friction of "I'll get to it" by doing the install yourself, in person, immediately.

### Fragility / judging larval startups
Early startups are extremely fragile — Airbnb's survival turned on roughly 30 days of in-person
engagement. Inexperienced observers misjudge a young startup by mature-company standards, "like
concluding that a newborn baby couldn't accomplish anything." The bigger danger is founders
dismissing their own startup's potential prematurely. (Microsoft began as a few people in
Albuquerque writing Basic interpreters.)

### Delight (insanely great experience)
Deliver customer service so attentive it seems pathological. Wufoo sent a hand-written thank-you
note to every new user. The principle, from Jobs: **"insanely great."** PG's refinement: **"It's
not the product that should be insanely great, but the experience of being your user."** An
incomplete early product can still deliver an extraordinary experience through founder
attentiveness — and that direct contact yields **the best feedback you will ever get**.

### Fire (deliberately narrow initial market)
Start with a market small enough to dominate and reach critical mass quickly. Facebook launched
only at Harvard, so it felt "naturally for them"; it then expanded college by college (with
manually-built course lists per school). Best case: build for yourself and your peers — knowing
your users intimately. Footnote: when choosing whom to target, prefer the fastest-converting users
(early adopters who improve the product) over the highest-paying.

### Meraki / hardware by hand
Hardware founders should assemble the first units themselves rather than waiting for factory
minimums (~$500K). Pebble hand-assembled the first several hundred watches and learned non-obvious
lessons (e.g., how valuable it is to source good screws). "Pulling a Meraki."

### Consult (treat one user as the whole market)
B2B founders can act almost as consultants to a single early user, perfecting the product for that
user's exact needs — solving one user's problem well often reveals the broader market. Stay a
**product company providing extreme attentiveness**, not a service company billing hours.

### Manual (do by hand what you'll later automate)
Do things manually behind the scenes and automate later. Stripe offered "instant" merchant
accounts by manually signing users up for conventional accounts in the background. Viaweb built
online stores on merchants' behalf when they wouldn't use the software themselves — which exposed
real pain points and missing features immediately. Operate by hand until a bottleneck makes the
automation target obvious.

### Big launch / partnerships are a myth
Coordinated simultaneous launches across outlets produce negligible results; growth comes from
satisfying the first small group of users, not broadcast. PG suspects an inverse correlation
between launch magnitude and success — the launches he remembers are famous flops (Segway, Google
Wave; Wave "a great idea... killed partly by its overdone launch"). Partnerships similarly don't
generate sustainable growth. (Google's relationship with Yahoo wasn't a partnership — Yahoo was a
customer.)

### Vector (the core mental model)
Treat a startup idea as a **vector**: (1) what you're going to build, plus (2) the unscalable thing
you'll do initially to get it going. Founders too often state only the first. The unscalable
component is real work and frequently becomes the company's lasting character. **Any strategy that
omits the effort is ipso facto suspect.**

### What founders find distasteful
Doing unscalable things feels uncomfortable, fake-professional founders imitate big-company flaws
(customer indifference) to seem legit — Garry Tan's point that embracing smallness' advantages is
actually more professional. But it's "less frightening than... building something automatic that
doesn't solve anyone's problems."

## Footnote substance (selection; numbering approximate)
- **Emerson correction:** the "beaten path to your door" line is misattributed/misquoted; Emerson
  actually wrote about good corn, wood, boards, pigs, chairs, knives, crucibles, or church organs.
- **Altman:** can't delegate early sales.
- **Patrick Collison:** "At some point, there was a very noticeable change in how Stripe felt. It
  tipped from being this boulder we had to push to being a train car that in fact had its own
  momentum."
- **User models are imperfect** because needs shift when new products appear (microcomputers
  created demand for spreadsheets).
- **Garry Tan:** founders imitate big-company indifference to seem professional — a trap.
- **Inverse launch correlation:** Segway, Google Wave as memorable failed launches.
- **Yahoo:** Google's customer, not a partner.
- **The "no second component" footnote (#f12n / ~note 12):** an idea where there is *nothing you
  can do to manually get going* — no path to find/recruit users — is probably a bad idea, at least
  for those founders. Reject ideas that have no viable unscalable launch vector.
