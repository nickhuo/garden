# Deliberately Narrow Initial Market

## Summary

The "Fire" move from [[Paul Graham]]'s essay: start with a market **small enough that you can dominate it and reach critical mass quickly**, then expand outward. A startup that lights a small, contained fire can build self-sustaining momentum before spreading; one that scatters effort across a broad market never reaches ignition.

## Worked example

**Facebook** launched only at Harvard, which made it feel like it was "naturally for them," then expanded **college by college** (with manually-built course lists per school). The narrowness was the feature, not a limitation.

## How to choose the market

- **Build for yourself and your peers** when possible — you then know your users intimately, and your product instincts are direct rather than inferred.
- When targeting users, prefer the **fastest-converting** ones (early adopters who improve the product and need little selling) over the **highest-paying** ones. Early-adopter feedback compounds; revenue can come later.
- User models are always imperfect because needs shift when new products appear — direct contact with a narrow group keeps the model fresh.

## Connections

- A core move under [[Do Things That Don't Scale]] — narrowness is often what makes the unscalable "second component" of the [[Startup Idea as Vector]] tractable: a market small enough that manual, one-by-one recruitment can actually reach critical mass.
