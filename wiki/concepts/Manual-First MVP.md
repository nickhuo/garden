# Manual-First MVP

## Summary

The "Manual" and "Consult" moves from [[Paul Graham]]'s essay: **do by hand what you will later automate.** Early on, the founders personally perform the operations that software is eventually supposed to do — often hidden behind a facade that looks automated to the user. (Often called the "concierge MVP" / "do-things-manually" pattern.)

## Worked examples

- **Stripe** offered "instant" merchant accounts — behind the scenes the founders *manually* signed users up for conventional merchant accounts.
- **Viaweb** (PG's own company) built online stores *for* merchants who wouldn't use the software themselves — which immediately exposed real pain points and missing features.
- **Consulting a single user** — treat one early customer as the whole market and perfect the product to their exact needs, while staying a product company (not a billed-hours service company). Solving one user's problem well often reveals the broader market.

## Why it works

- You ship and learn far faster than if you waited to automate everything.
- Doing the work by hand builds **muscle memory for exactly what to automate** — the bottleneck reveals itself, so you automate the right thing instead of guessing.
- It is "a little frightening... but less frightening than... building something automatic that doesn't solve anyone's problems."

## Connections

- A core technique under [[Do Things That Don't Scale]]; the canonical "second component" in the [[Startup Idea as Vector]] model.
- Sibling of [[Collison Installation]] (manual onboarding) — both are founders personally doing what software will later do.
- Rhymes with the concierge/manual-operations stage that precedes automation in agent products; the design question of *what to automate first* mirrors finding the bottleneck by hand.
