# Startup Idea as Vector

## Summary

[[Paul Graham]]'s mental model for stating a startup idea correctly. An idea is not just *what you build* — it's a **vector** with two components:

1. **What you're going to build.**
2. **The unscalable thing(s) you'll do initially to get it going.**

Founders habitually state only the first component. The second is real, hard work and is just as load-bearing. **"Any strategy that omits the effort is ipso facto suspect."**

## Why the second component matters

- It forces you to plan the [[Do Things That Don't Scale]] bootstrap up front, instead of assuming users will arrive.
- The unscalable component frequently becomes the company's **permanent character** — aggressive acquisition, manufacturing know-how, or customer obsession that persists at scale.
- It is a **filter on ideas**: per the essay's footnote (anchor `#f12n`), an idea with *no* viable second component — nothing you can do to manually find and recruit users — is probably a bad idea, at least for those founders. The absence of a launch vector is a red flag, not a detail to figure out later.

## Connections

- The formalization of [[Do Things That Don't Scale]].
- [[Manual-First MVP]] and [[Collison Installation]] are concrete instances of "the second component."
- [[Deliberately Narrow Initial Market]] is often *how* the second component is made tractable (a small enough market that manual recruitment can reach critical mass).
