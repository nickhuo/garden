# 2013-07 - Paul Graham - Do Things that Don't Scale

> [!key-insight] One-line
> Startups don't take off by themselves — **the founders make them take off** — and the way they do it early is by deliberately doing labor-intensive, *unscalable* things that later become the company's DNA.

## Summary

Paul Graham's canonical 2013 essay (see [[Paul Graham]], [[Y Combinator]]) argues that the most common reason would-be founders fail is the false belief that a good product attracts users on its own. In reality the founders have to generate the initial momentum by hand. The essay catalogs the unscalable moves: **recruit users manually**, **delight them with pathological attentiveness**, **start in a deliberately narrow market**, **assemble hardware / do operations by hand**, and **consult a single user as if they were the whole market**. The unifying mental model is the [[Startup Idea as Vector]]: an idea is *what you build* **plus** *the unscalable thing you'll do to get going* — and a strategy that omits the second component is suspect.

For the durable ideas, see [[Do Things That Don't Scale]] (umbrella), [[Startup Idea as Vector]], [[Collison Installation]], [[Manual-First MVP]], and [[Deliberately Narrow Initial Market]].

## Key moves (the essay's catalog)

- **Recruit.** Go get users one at a time; you can't delegate early sales (Altman). The "beaten path to your door" / better-mousetrap assumption is false.
- **Delight.** Service so attentive it seems unscalable — Wufoo's hand-written thank-you notes. *"It's not the product that should be insanely great, but the experience of being your user."* Direct contact yields the best feedback you'll ever get.
- **Fire (narrow market).** Dominate a small market first — Facebook launched only at Harvard. Build for yourself and your peers. → [[Deliberately Narrow Initial Market]].
- **Meraki (hardware by hand).** Assemble the first units yourself rather than wait for ~$500K factory minimums — Pebble hand-built hundreds of watches.
- **Consult.** Treat one early user as the entire market; perfect the product for them, stay a product company not a billed-hours service company.
- **Manual.** Do by hand what you'll later automate — Stripe's "instant" merchant accounts were manual signups behind the scenes; Viaweb built stores for merchants. → [[Manual-First MVP]].
- **No big launch.** Launch magnitude doesn't drive growth (PG suspects inverse correlation — Segway, Google Wave); satisfying the first small user base does. Partnerships don't either.

## Named examples

| Company / person | Illustrates |
|---|---|
| **Stripe / Collison brothers** | Aggressive manual recruitment + the [[Collison Installation]] ("Right then, give me your laptop"); manual merchant accounts behind an "instant" facade |
| **Airbnb** | Door-to-door recruitment in NYC; ~30 days of in-person engagement decided survival |
| **Pinterest** (Ben Silbermann) | Recruited early users in person from design conferences |
| **Wufoo** | Hand-written thank-you notes — pathological early delight |
| **Pebble / Meraki** | Hand-assembling hardware before scaling; "pulling a Meraki" |
| **Viaweb** (PG's own) | Built online stores for merchants who wouldn't use the software — exposed real needs |
| **Facebook** | Deliberately narrow start (Harvard only), then college-by-college expansion |
| **Microsoft** | "Larval" beginnings — a few people writing Basic in Albuquerque |
| **Google / Yahoo** | Yahoo was a *customer*, not a partner — partnerships don't drive growth |
